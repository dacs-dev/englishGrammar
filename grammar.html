Pronouns

Pronouns

🔹 Nota: Los pronombres no se modifican añadiendo “-s”, “-es” u otras terminaciones. Su forma es fija y depende de su función en la oración.

Personal Pronouns:

  • I, you, he, she, it, we, they

Object Pronouns:

  • me, you, him, her, it, us, them

Possessive Adjectives:

  • my, your, his, her, its, our, their
  • Example: This is my book.

Possessive Pronouns:

  • mine, yours, his, hers, its*, ours, theirs
  • Example: This book is mine.
  • *"its" as possessive pronoun is rarely used

Reflexive Pronouns:

  • myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves

Present Simple + State & Action Verbs

Present Simple + State & Action Verbs

Structure: Subject + base verb (add -s/-es/-ies for he/she/it)

  • I work / He works
  • They play / She plays

Use:

  • Habits and routines (e.g. I brush my teeth every day)
  • General truths (e.g. The sun rises in the east)
  • Permanent situations (e.g. He lives in Madrid)
  • Timetables (e.g. The train leaves at 8 a.m.)

State verbs (non-action): Used with Present Simple, not normally with -ing

  • like, love, hate, prefer
  • believe, know, understand, remember
  • need, want, belong, own, seem

✅ I know the answer — ❌ I am knowing the answer

Action verbs: Describe physical/observable actions. Used in all tenses.

  • run, eat, play, write, read, cook, speak

Spelling rules for he/she/it:

Verb type Rule Example
Regular verb +s play → plays
-s, -sh, -ch, -x, -o +es go → goes
Consonant + y y → ies study → studies
Vowel + y +s play → plays

No -s for: I, you, we, they → They read books.

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ She go to school → ✅ She goes to school
  • ❌ I am know him → ✅ I know him

Present Continuous

Present Continuous

Structure: Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing

Pronoun Form
I am working
You / We / They are playing
He / She / It is reading

Use:

  • Actions happening now → She is talking on the phone.
  • Temporary situations → I’m staying with my cousin this week.
  • Changes/developments → The climate is getting warmer.
  • Repeated annoying actions → He is always complaining.

State verbs: Normally not used in -ing form

  • know, believe, understand, remember
  • want, like, love, hate, need
  • belong, own, seem

❌ I am knowing → ✅ I know

Spelling rules for -ing:

Case Rule Example
Ends in -e Drop -e, add -ing write → writing
1 vowel + 1 consonant Double consonant run → running
2 vowels/consonants Just add -ing read → reading
Ends in -ie Change to -ying die → dying

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He working → ✅ He is working
  • ❌ She is read → ✅ She is reading
  • ❌ We are goes → ✅ We are going

Present Perfect Simple

Present Perfect Simple

Structure: Subject + have/has + past participle

Pronoun Form
I / You / We / They have worked
He / She / It has gone

Use:

  • Experiences → I have visited London.
  • Present relevance → I have lost my keys.
  • Recent actions (just) → He has just arrived.
  • Unfinished time (for/since) → They have lived here for 10 years.

Common adverbs: ever, never, just, already, yet, still, for, since, recently, lately

Past participle formation:

  • Regular: base + -ed → worked, lived
  • Irregular: learned forms → gone, eaten, seen

Word order with adverbs:

Adverb Position Example
ever / never Between aux & participle Have you ever seen it?
just / already Same She has just arrived.
yet End (questions/negatives) Have you eaten yet?

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ I have went → ✅ I have gone
  • ❌ She has eat → ✅ She has eaten
  • ❌ Did you ever see it? → ✅ Have you ever seen it?

Present Perfect Continuous

Present Perfect Continuous

Structure: Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing

Pronoun Form
I / You / We / They have been working
He / She / It has been studying

Use:

  • To emphasize the duration of an activity → We have been working for 3 hours.
  • To describe recent activities with visible results → You look tired. Have you been running?
  • To express temporary actions → He has been staying with friends.

Common time expressions: for, since, lately, recently, all day, how long?

Spelling notes for verb-ing:

  • drop -e → come → coming
  • double consonant → run → running
  • -ie → -ying → die → dying

Differences from Present Perfect Simple:

  • Perfect Continuous = focus on duration → I have been reading for 2 hours.
  • Perfect Simple = focus on result → I have read 100 pages.

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ She has being studying → ✅ She has been studying
  • ❌ We have been wait → ✅ We have been waiting
  • ❌ He has been works → ✅ He has been working

Past Time & Present Perfect

Past Time vs Present Perfect Simple

Past Simple: Subject + past verb (regular +ed / irregular form)

Present Perfect: Subject + have/has + past participle

🕒 Use Past Simple:

  • Completed actions in the past with a clear time → I went to Paris in 2020.
  • Finished periods → She worked there last year.

🕒 Use Present Perfect:

  • Actions with no specific time → He has visited Rome.
  • Life experience → Have you ever seen a whale?
  • Actions that affect the present → I’ve lost my phone (I don’t have it now).

Time markers:

Past Simple Present Perfect
yesterday, last week, in 2010, when I was 10 ever, never, already, just, yet, for, since, recently

⚠️ Key differences:

  • Past Simple = When? ✔️ → She left an hour ago.
  • Present Perfect = When? ❌ → She has left.

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ I have seen her yesterday → ✅ I saw her yesterday
  • ❌ Did you ever go to Italy? → ✅ Have you ever gone to Italy?
  • ❌ He has finished his homework last night → ✅ He finished it last night

Past Continuous

Past Continuous

Structure: Subject + was/were + verb-ing

Pronoun Form
I / He / She / It was working
You / We / They were studying

Use:

  • Ongoing actions in the past → She was reading at 9 p.m.
  • Background actions in a story → The sun was shining and birds were singing.
  • Interrupted actions (often with “when” or “while”) → I was cooking when the phone rang.

Time markers: while, as, when, all day, at that moment, all morning, etc.

Spelling rules for -ing:

  • stop → stopping (double consonant)
  • come → coming (drop -e)
  • die → dying (-ie → -ying)

Past Continuous vs Past Simple:

  • Past Continuous = longer/ongoing action
  • Past Simple = shorter/interruption
  • I was watching TV when the lights went out.

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He were playing → ✅ He was playing
  • ❌ We was study → ✅ We were studying
  • ❌ I was to cook → ✅ I was cooking

Past Perfect

Past Perfect

Structure: Subject + had + past participle

  • I had finished before they arrived.
  • She had already left when we got there.

Use:

  • To describe an action completed before another past action → He had left before the movie started.
  • To express cause/effect in the past → She was tired because she had worked all night.
  • To give background or clarify sequence in storytelling → I had never seen snow before that day.

Common time markers:

  • before, after, by the time, already, just, never, when, as soon as, until then

Past Perfect vs Past Simple:

  • Past Perfect = happened first → They had eaten
  • Past Simple = happened second → when I arrived.
  • Example: They had finished dinner when I arrived.

Negative form: had + not + past participle → She hadn’t called me.

Question form: Had + subject + past participle? → Had you studied?

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He didn’t had lunch → ✅ He hadn’t had lunch
  • ❌ Did you had dinner? → ✅ Had you had dinner?
  • ❌ I had went home → ✅ I had gone home

Future: Will & Going to

Future Time: Will & Going to

Will: Subject + will + base verb

Going to: Subject + am/is/are + going to + base verb

✅ Use will for:

  • Spontaneous decisions → I’ll help you!
  • Promises, offers, threats → I won’t forget.
  • Predictions (opinion-based) → I think it will rain.

✅ Use going to for:

  • Planned actions/intentions → I’m going to study tonight.
  • Evidence-based predictions → Look at those clouds! It’s going to rain.

Examples:

  • She’s going to visit her grandmother next weekend.
  • He’ll call you as soon as he arrives.
  • I’m not going to buy it. It’s too expensive.

Time expressions: tomorrow, next week, in 2030, soon, later, tonight, in a minute

Negative form:

  • will not (won’t) → I won’t go
  • am/is/are not going to → She isn’t going to study

Questions:

  • Will you come?
  • Are they going to travel?

Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He will going → ✅ He will go
  • ❌ I am going to will buy it → ✅ I am going to buy it
  • ❌ Are you will help me? → ✅ Will you help me?

Future Continuous,
Future Perfect & Continuous

Future Continuous, Future Perfect & Future Perfect Continuous

📌 Future Continuous

Structure: will + be + verb-ing

  • Action in progress at a future time → This time tomorrow, I’ll be flying to London.
  • Polite/neutral questions about plans → Will you be joining us later?

📌 Future Perfect Simple

Structure: will + have + past participle

  • Action completed before a point in the future → I will have finished the report by Monday.
  • Often used with “by”, “before”, or specific deadlines

📌 Future Perfect Continuous

Structure: will + have + been + verb-ing

  • Focus on duration of an action until a future moment → By July, I’ll have been working here for 10 years.

⏱ Time markers:

  • Future Continuous: this time tomorrow, at 5 p.m., in a few hours
  • Future Perfect: by next week, before Sunday, by the end of the month
  • Future Perfect Continuous: for, since, by then, for over two hours

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ I will be work tomorrow → ✅ I will be working
  • ❌ She will has finished → ✅ She will have finished
  • ❌ He will have be studying → ✅ He will have been studying

Adverbs & Adjectives:
yet, still, already, for & since

Adverbs & Adjectives: yet, still, already, for & since

🔹 yet

  • Used in questions & negatives only
  • Placed at the end of the sentence
  • Have you finished your homework yet?
  • I haven’t seen it yet.

🔹 already

  • Used in affirmative sentences and some questions
  • Goes between the auxiliary and the past participle
  • She has already eaten lunch.
  • Have you already been there?

🔹 still

  • Used in affirmatives and negatives to show something continues
  • Goes before the main verb
  • He still lives with his parents.
  • She still hasn’t replied.

🔹 for & since

  • Used with Present Perfect or Past Perfect
  • for + period of time → I’ve lived here for 10 years.
  • since + starting point → She’s been here since 2015.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ I already have eaten → ✅ I have already eaten
  • ❌ Have you yet finished? → ✅ Have you finished yet?
  • ❌ I’ve been here since 3 years → ✅ I’ve been here for 3 years
  • ❌ He still didn’t arrive → ✅ He still hasn’t arrived

Connectors

Connectors (Linking Words)

Definition: Connectors are words or phrases that join ideas, sentences or clauses. They help create cohesion and coherence.

🔸 Addition

  • and, also, too, as well as, in addition, moreover, furthermore
  • She sings and dances.
  • In addition, she plays the guitar.

🔸 Contrast

  • but, although, though, however, whereas, nevertheless, on the other hand
  • Although it was cold, we went swimming.
  • She’s friendly, whereas he’s very shy.

🔸 Cause & Reason

  • because, since, as, due to, owing to
  • They left early because it was raining.
  • Due to the snow, school was cancelled.

🔸 Result & Consequence

  • so, therefore, as a result, consequently, thus
  • He was late, so he missed the train.
  • She worked hard; therefore, she passed.

🔸 Purpose

  • to, in order to, so that, for the purpose of
  • He studies to improve his English.
  • She left early so that she could catch the bus.

🔸 Time

  • when, while, after, before, until, as soon as, since
  • Call me when you arrive.
  • She has worked here since 2010.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ Because she was tired, so she went to bed → ✅ Because she was tired, she went to bed.
  • ❌ He worked hard, although he failed → ✅ He worked hard, but he failed.
  • ❌ Since 3 years → ✅ For 3 years (use since + point in time)

Comparatives & Superlatives

Comparatives & Superlatives

📌 Comparatives

Use: Compare two things → Tom is taller than Jack.

  • Short adjectives (1 syllable): +er → fast → faster
  • Adjectives ending in -y: y → ier → happy → happier
  • Long adjectives (2+ syllables): more + adj → more expensive

📌 Superlatives

Use: Highlight the highest/lowest degree among 3+ → She is the fastest runner.

  • Short adjectives: the + adj + est → small → the smallest
  • Ending in -y: y → iest → happy → the happiest
  • Long adjectives: the most + adj → the most beautiful

🧠 Irregular adjectives:

Adjective Comparative Superlative
good better the best
bad worse the worst
far farther/further the farthest/furthest

📝 Notes:

  • Use than after comparatives → smarter than me
  • Use the before superlatives → the most interesting
  • For equality: as + adj + as → He is as tall as his brother.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ more tall → ✅ taller
  • ❌ the most fastest → ✅ the fastest
  • ❌ better than her is → ✅ better than her

Prepositions

Prepositions

Definition: Prepositions are words used before nouns or pronouns to show relation in time, place, direction, cause, manner, etc.

📍 Place

  • in: inside an enclosed space → in the room
  • on: on a surface → on the table
  • at: specific point → at the station
  • under, above, next to, between, behind

⏰ Time

  • in: months, years, centuries → in July, in 2020
  • on: specific days/dates → on Monday, on May 5th
  • at: specific times → at 3 p.m., at night
  • Other: before, after, since, for, during, until

🏃 Movement/Direction

  • to: direction → go to school
  • into: entering → walk into the room
  • onto: movement to a surface → jump onto the bed
  • out of, off, across, along, through

🎯 Other Common Prepositions

  • about, by, with, without, for, from, of
  • This book is about history. / A gift for you.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ at Monday → ✅ on Monday
  • ❌ in 5 p.m. → ✅ at 5 p.m.
  • ❌ go in school → ✅ go to school
  • ❌ married with him → ✅ married to him

Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal Verbs

Definition: A phrasal verb is a verb combined with a preposition or adverb (or both), forming a meaning different from the original verb.

📌 Structure:

Verb + particle (preposition or adverb)
e.g. give up, look after, run into

📚 Types:

  • Transitive: need an object → She put off the meeting.
  • Intransitive: no object → He woke up late.
  • Separable: object can go between → Turn off the light / Turn the light off.
  • Inseparable: object must go after → Look after the baby.

⭐ Common Phrasal Verbs:

  • get up: rise from bed
  • give up: stop trying
  • look after: take care of
  • run into: meet by chance
  • turn on/off: start/stop a device
  • find out: discover
  • put off: postpone
  • break down: stop working (machine)

📍 Position of object:

  • Separable: She turned off the radio. / She turned the radio off.
  • Inseparable: He looks after his brother. ❌ Not: *He looks his brother after.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He gave the homework up → ✅ He gave up the homework
  • ❌ I look my dog after → ✅ I look after my dog
  • ❌ She turned off it → ✅ She turned it off

Conditionals
Zero, First, Second & Third

Conditionals: Zero, First, Second & Third

Structure: if-clause + main clause (condition + result)

🔹 Zero Conditional

  • Form: If + present simple, present simple
  • Use: general truths, scientific facts
  • If you heat water, it boils.

🔹 First Conditional

  • Form: If + present simple, will + base verb
  • Use: real, likely future situations
  • If it rains, we’ll stay inside.

🔹 Second Conditional

  • Form: If + past simple, would + base verb
  • Use: imaginary/unreal situations in present or future
  • If I had a car, I would drive to work.

🔹 Third Conditional

  • Form: If + past perfect, would have + past participle
  • Use: unreal past situations, regrets
  • If she had studied, she would have passed.

📍 Notes:

  • “If” can sometimes be replaced with “when” in Zero Conditional
  • You can reverse clause order → We’ll stay inside if it rains.
  • Use “were” instead of “was” in Second Conditional → If I were you…

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ If I will see her, I tell her → ✅ If I see her, I’ll tell her
  • ❌ If I would be rich → ✅ If I were rich
  • ❌ If I studied, I would have passed → ✅ If I had studied, I would have passed

Mixed Conditionals

Mixed Conditionals

Definition: Mixed conditionals combine two different time references (past ↔ present/future). They are used when the condition and result refer to different times.

🔹 Type 1: Past condition → present result

  • Form: If + past perfect, would + base verb
  • Use: to show how a past action affects the present
  • If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now.

🔹 Type 2: Present condition → past result

  • Form: If + past simple, would have + past participle
  • Use: to imagine a present situation affecting a past outcome
  • If I were taller, I would have joined the basketball team.

📝 Examples:

  • If she had listened to me, she wouldn’t be in trouble now.
  • If we weren’t so tired, we would have gone out last night.

📍 Notes:

  • Only the condition or the result shifts in time, not both
  • Very useful to express regret or hypothetical analysis
  • Used frequently in real-world conversations

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ If I had more time, I would have studied → ✅ This is NOT mixed, it's incorrect mix
  • ❌ If I was rich, I would have been happy → ✅ If I were rich, I would have been happy
  • ❌ If I had met her, I will be happy → ✅ If I had met her, I would be happy

Modal Verbs
Obligation & Ability

Modal Verbs: Obligation & Ability

Definition: Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, permission, or ability. They are followed by the base form of the verb (without “to”).

🟩 Obligation

  • must: strong obligation (speaker’s opinion) → You must wear a seatbelt.
  • have to: external rule/law → Students have to wear uniforms.
  • need to: necessity (less strong than must) → You need to drink more water.
  • should / ought to: advice → You should exercise more.

📉 Lack of Obligation

  • don’t have to: no obligation → You don’t have to come if you’re busy.
  • needn’t: not necessary → You needn’t worry.

⛔ Prohibition

  • mustn’t: not allowed → You mustn’t smoke here.
  • can’t: forbidden by rule or logic → You can’t drive without a license.

🟦 Ability

  • can: present/future ability → She can swim.
  • could: past general ability → I could read at age 5.
  • be able to: all tenses → He will be able to help.

🕒 Past Modal Forms

  • had to: past obligation → We had to leave early.
  • could: general ability in the past
  • was/were able to: specific past success → I was able to fix it.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He musts study → ✅ He must study
  • ❌ She can to swim → ✅ She can swim
  • ❌ I don’t must go → ✅ I mustn’t go / I don’t have to go (⚠️ different meanings)

Reported Speech
Direct & Indirect Style

Reported Speech: Direct & Indirect Style

Definition: Reported (Indirect) Speech is used to express what someone said without quoting their exact words.

🗣️ Direct vs Indirect:

  • Direct: “I’m tired,” she said.
  • Indirect: She said (that) she was tired.

🔄 Changes in Tense:

(Only if reporting verb is in past)

Direct Indirect
Present Simple Past Simple
Present Continuous Past Continuous
Past Simple Past Perfect
Present Perfect Past Perfect
Will Would
Can Could

📍 Other Changes:

  • Pronouns: “I” → he/she
  • Time expressions: today → that day, now → then, tomorrow → the next day
  • Place: here → there

🔧 Reporting Verbs:

  • say, tell, ask, explain, add, promise, warn
  • She told me (that) she was tired.
  • He asked if I had seen the film.

✅ No tense change when:

  • Reporting verb is in the present
  • The statement is still true
  • She says she’s tired.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ He said me → ✅ He told me / He said (that)
  • ❌ She said that she is happy → ✅ She said that she was happy
  • ❌ He told that he went → ✅ He said that / He told me that

Relative Clauses
Defining & Non-defining

Relative Clauses: Defining & Non-defining

Definition: Relative clauses give extra information about a noun using a relative pronoun (who, which, that, etc.).

🔹 Defining Relative Clauses

  • Essential to the meaning of the sentence
  • No commas
  • The woman who lives next door is a doctor.
  • Relative pronouns: who, which, that, where, whose

🔹 Non-defining Relative Clauses

  • Give extra (non-essential) information
  • Always use commas
  • “That” is not allowed
  • My brother, who lives in Madrid, is visiting me.

📌 Relative Pronouns:

  • who: for people → The man who called you
  • which: for things/animals → The book which I bought
  • that: for people/things (only in defining clauses)
  • whose: possession → The girl whose phone was stolen
  • where: for places → The town where I grew up
  • when: for time → The year when we met

📍 Omitting the pronoun:

  • Possible in defining clauses if the pronoun is object → The film (that) we saw was great.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ My friend which lives in London → ✅ My friend who lives in London
  • ❌ The car, that I bought → ✅ The car, which I bought
  • ❌ The book that I read, was amazing → ✅ No comma before “that”

Passive Voice
Active & Passive

Passive Voice: Active & Passive

Definition: The passive voice is used when the focus is on the action or the receiver of the action, rather than the doer.

🔁 Form:

Passive = be (in the correct tense) + past participle (V3)

🕒 Tense Transformation:

Active Passive
He writes a letter A letter is written (by him)
He is writing a letter A letter is being written
He wrote a letter A letter was written
He has written a letter A letter has been written
He will write a letter A letter will be written
He had written a letter A letter had been written

👤 Agent (“by...”):

  • Use “by” to mention the doer: The cake was made by Mary.
  • Often omitted if not important or unknown: The window was broken.

📌 Common Uses:

  • When the doer is unknown or irrelevant
  • In formal or scientific writing → The experiment was conducted...
  • To sound more objective or impersonal

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ The book wrote by her → ✅ The book was written by her
  • ❌ Is cleaned the room → ✅ The room is cleaned
  • ❌ The door is open by him → ✅ The door was opened by him

Intensifiers
So, Such, Too, Enough

Intensifiers: So, Such, Too, Enough

Definition: Intensifiers are words that make adjectives, adverbs, or nouns stronger or more emphatic.

🔹 So

  • Structure: so + adjective/adverb → The book is so interesting.
  • Use for emphasis
  • so...that: for result → She was so tired that she fell asleep.

🔹 Such

  • Structure: such + (adjective) + noun → It was such a nice day.
  • Use before nouns (with or without adjective)
  • such...that: → It was such a bad film that we left early.

🔹 Too

  • Structure: too + adjective/adverb → It’s too cold to go out.
  • Negative meaning: more than necessary or desirable
  • too...to: → She’s too young to drive.

🔹 Enough

  • Structure: adjective/adverb + enough OR enough + noun
  • She isn’t tall enough to reach. / We have enough money.
  • Means "sufficient"

⚖️ Comparisons:

  • so → intensifies adjectives/adverbs
  • such → intensifies noun phrases
  • too → implies a problem
  • enough → indicates sufficiency

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ It was so good film → ✅ It was such a good film
  • ❌ She is too much tired → ✅ She is too tired
  • ❌ He isn’t enough strong → ✅ He isn’t strong enough

Question Tags

Question Tags

Definition: A question tag is a short question added at the end of a statement to confirm information or ask for agreement.

🔁 Structure:

Statement + auxiliary/modal verb + subject pronoun + ?

  • Positive statement → negative tag → You're coming, aren't you?
  • Negative statement → positive tag → He isn't here, is he?

📌 Rules:

  • Use the same auxiliary/modal verb as in the sentence
  • If there is no auxiliary, use do/does/did
  • Use pronouns, not nouns, in the tag → Anna is nice, isn't she?

🎧 Intonation:

  • 🔼 Rising → genuine question (uncertain)
  • 🔽 Falling → confirmation (expecting agreement)

🔧 Examples:

  • She works hard, doesn’t she?
  • They didn’t go, did they?
  • You can drive, can’t you?
  • Let’s go, shall we?
  • I’m late, aren’t I? ✅ (⚠️ Exception!)

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ She’s nice, is she? → ✅ She’s nice, isn’t she?
  • ❌ He don’t like it, doesn’t he? → ✅ He doesn’t like it, does he?
  • ❌ I’m late, am I not? → ✅ I’m late, aren’t I

I Wish / If Only

I Wish / If Only

Definition: “I wish” and “If only” express regret, desire for change, or complaints about a situation. They are followed by a verb tense that reflects the opposite of the reality.

🔹 1. Wishes about the present (unreal situations)

  • Structure: I wish / If only + past simple
  • I wish I had more money. / If only she knew the answer.
  • Use: things we would like to be different now

🔹 2. Wishes about the past (regrets)

  • Structure: I wish / If only + past perfect
  • I wish I had studied more. / If only we hadn’t missed the bus.
  • Use: regrets about things that already happened

🔹 3. Wishes about the future (unlikely desires)

  • Structure: I wish / If only + would + verb
  • I wish it would stop raining. / If only they would listen.
  • Use: to express annoyance or desire for a change in someone’s behavior

📍 Notes:

  • If only is more emphatic than “I wish”
  • “were” is preferred over “was” in formal English → I wish I were taller.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ I wish I have a car → ✅ I wish I had a car
  • ❌ If only he comes → ✅ If only he would come
  • ❌ I wish I would know → ✅ I wish I knew

Prepositional Verbs

Prepositional Verbs

Definition: A prepositional verb is a verb that is followed by a specific preposition before its object.

🧩 Structure:

Verb + preposition + object

  • She believes in hard work.
  • He apologized for the mistake.

📌 Common Prepositional Verbs:

  • believe in → I believe in you.
  • depend on → It depends on the weather.
  • agree with → I agree with your point.
  • apologize for → She apologized for being late.
  • belong to → That car belongs to my uncle.
  • focus on → Let’s focus on the task.
  • talk about → We talked about the problem.
  • laugh at → Don’t laugh at others.
  • listen to → I like listening to music.
  • wait for → I'm waiting for the bus.

⚠️ Important Notes:

  • The preposition is necessary — removing it changes the meaning or makes it incorrect.
  • The preposition never separates from the verb.
  • It is not the same as a phrasal verb (which can change the meaning entirely).

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ She listened music → ✅ She listened to music
  • ❌ I depend him → ✅ I depend on him
  • ❌ We talked the plan → ✅ We talked about the plan

Prepositional Phrases

Prepositional Phrases

Definition: A prepositional phrase is a group of words starting with a preposition and ending with a noun or pronoun (the object of the preposition).

📌 Structure:

Preposition + noun/pronoun (object)

  • in the morning
  • at the station
  • under the bed

🔹 Types of Prepositional Phrases:

  • Time: at night, in 2005, during lunch
  • Place: under the table, at school, near the park
  • Cause, purpose or reason: because of the rain, due to stress
  • Manner: with enthusiasm, in a hurry
  • Instrument: by hand, with a key
  • Comparison: in contrast to, similar to

📖 Examples in Context:

  • He arrived at the airport.
  • She sat on the bench.
  • We stayed indoors because of the storm.

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ I arrived to the station → ✅ I arrived at the station
  • ❌ She was angry to him → ✅ She was angry with him
  • ❌ We discussed about the topic → ✅ We discussed the topic

✅ Some verbs include the preposition as part of their structure. Always learn prepositions with their verbs or phrases.

Verb Phrases

Verb Phrases

Definition: A verb phrase is a group of words made up of a main verb and one or more auxiliary (helping) verbs.

🔧 Structure:

Auxiliary/modal verb(s) + main verb

  • She is studying for the exam.
  • We have been waiting for hours.
  • I can speak English fluently.

📌 Types of Verb Phrases:

  • Simple: just the main verb → They play tennis.
  • Progressive: be + verb-ing → She is running.
  • Perfect: have + past participle → We have eaten.
  • Passive: be + past participle → The book was written.
  • Modal: modal + base verb → He might come.
  • Combined: modal + have + been + verb-ing → She may have been working.

🧠 Use & Meaning:

  • To indicate tense, aspect, voice, modality
  • To build more complex meanings (possibility, obligation, continuity, etc.)

⚠️ Common mistakes:

  • ❌ She can sings → ✅ She can sing
  • ❌ We have went → ✅ We have gone
  • ❌ He is play → ✅ He is playing
  • ❌ They was watching → ✅ They were watching

💡 Tip: Always learn verb phrases in context and pay attention to auxiliary combinations.
